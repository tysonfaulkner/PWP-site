## Introduction

Precise pricing is essential for roofing business success. Setting rates too low risks financial losses, while excessive charges drive away clients. The key is balancing project scope and labor hours to overhead costs and your desired profit margin.

## Understanding the Project Scope

Before pricing, thoroughly assess what the job entails:

- **Roof Size**: Measured in squares (100 square feet per square)
- **Roof Pitch**: Steeper roofs require more time and labor
- **Roof Type**: Shingles, metal, or other materials have different installation requirements
- **Existing Roof Condition**: Determining removal needs and repairs affects overall costs

Take detailed notes and photographs to ensure your crew understands the full scope.

## Calculating Material Costs

Material expenses typically represent the largest project cost:

- Order 5-10% extra to account for mistakes and waste
- Account for shingles, underlayment, nails, flashing, vents, drip edges, and ridge caps
- Premium materials cost more but offer better durability and warranties
- Explore bulk pricing discounts from suppliers

Using spreadsheets or specialized software helps track every item accurately.

## Estimating Labor Expenses

Labor costs cover crew time for tearoff, installation, cleanup, and extra work. Two common approaches:

### Hourly Pay

Fixed rate per hour. Requires estimating total project duration. Simple to track but can be harder to predict final costs if the job runs longer than expected.

### Piece Work

Paying crews per unit completed (like per square installed), which incentivizes faster completion. With a piece work method, you pay your crew for the amount of work completed, such as a set amount per square of shingles installed. This gives you predictable per-unit labor costs. ## Considering Overhead and Profit

Beyond materials and labor, factor in:

### Overhead Expenses

- Office rent or utilities
- Equipment maintenance
- Insurance
- Vehicles and fuel
- Administration costs

Calculate daily overhead expenses and multiply by the estimated project duration. Use our [job duration estimator](/tools/job-duration-estimator) to plan timelines, and our [revenue calculator](/tools/revenue-calculator) to project monthly and annual revenue. A typical construction profit margin ranges from 10-20%.

## Using a Piece Work Approach

Piece work systems provide clearer labor cost visibility while motivating employee productivity. Proper tracking requires dedicated systems that allow:

- Clock in/clock-out functionality
- Daily piece work entry logging
- Time card approval workflows
- Payroll report generation
- Job cost analysis

These tools support hourly, piece work, or mixed payment models.

## Tracking and Adjusting in Real Time

Monitor progress continuously:

- Check daily progress and address delays immediately
- Compare actual costs against initial estimates
- Adjust pricing for mid-project changes
- Refine future bids based on actual performance data

Real-time monitoring prevents small problems from becoming major financial issues.
