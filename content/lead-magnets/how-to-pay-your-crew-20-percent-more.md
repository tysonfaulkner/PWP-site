## Part 1: The Number That's Bleeding You Dry

Most contractors know two numbers: what they charge and what they pay. You bid a roofing job at $350 a square and pay your guys $45 a square. That's $305 in gross margin. Easy money, right?

Not even close.

That $45 per square is just the start of what that worker actually costs you. On top of every dollar you pay in wages, you owe:

- **FICA (Social Security & Medicare):** 7.65%
- **Federal Unemployment (FUTA):** 0.6%
- **State Unemployment (SUTA):** 2–6% depending on your state and claims history
- **Workers' Compensation:** 5–30%+ depending on trade and state (roofing is one of the highest)
- **General Liability Insurance:** varies, but it's a real cost per labor dollar
- **Equipment, vehicles, and tools** your crew uses on the job

Add it all up, and that $45/square worker is actually costing you **$56 to $65 per square** when you factor in the full labor burden.

**That's 25–45% more than the number on the check you hand them.**

Most contractors bid against the base rate. The profitable ones bid against the burdened rate.

### The Burdened Rate Formula

Here's the simple version:

**True Labor Cost = Base Piece Rate × (1 + Burden Percentage)**

If your burden rate is 35% and you pay $45/square:

$45 × 1.35 = **$60.75 actual cost per square**

That changes your margin math completely:

- **What you thought:** $350 - $45 = $305 margin
- **What's real:** $350 - $60.75 = $289.25 margin (per worker on the crew)

Multiply that gap across a 4-man crew doing 20+ squares a day, across hundreds of jobs a year, and you start to see where the money goes.

> **Action step:** Calculate your actual burden percentage. Add up every employer-paid cost — taxes, insurance, comp, benefits — and divide by total wages paid last year. Write that number down. You'll need it for everything that follows.

## Part 2: Why Paying More Actually Makes You More

This is the part that feels backwards until you see it play out.

Let's say you've got two crews. Crew A is your B-team — decent guys, decent speed, $40/square. Crew B is your A-team — fast, clean, experienced, $50/square.

At first glance, Crew A looks cheaper. But watch what happens on a real job:

### Crew A ($40/square)

- 4 guys, average 15 squares/day
- Job is 60 squares = 4 days on-site
- Total piece rate labor: 60 × $40 = $2,400
- Burdened at 35%: $3,240
- Callbacks for punch list items: 1 trip back, half day = $480 in wages + burden
- **True labor cost: $3,720**

### Crew B ($50/square)

- 4 guys, average 22 squares/day
- Same job, 60 squares = 3 days on-site
- Total piece rate labor: 60 × $50 = $3,000
- Burdened at 35%: $4,050
- Callbacks: zero — they do it right the first time
- **True labor cost: $4,050**

Crew B costs $330 more in direct labor. But here's what you're not seeing:

**Crew A burned 4 days of your schedule. Crew B burned 3.** That extra day means:

- One less job that week for that crew slot
- Your fixed overhead (truck, insurance, rent, your salary) spread across fewer jobs
- A callback trip that costs fuel, time, and reputation

If Crew B's extra day lets you squeeze in even **one more job per month**, that's $5,000–$15,000 in additional revenue you'd never have seen.

**Paying $10 more per square cost you $330 on this job. The extra job next week made you $8,000.**

That's the math the busiest-but-brokest contractors never run.

### The Retention Multiplier

There's a cost most contractors never calculate: **what it costs when a good worker leaves.**

Think about what happens when your best guy walks:

- 2–4 weeks to find a replacement (if you're lucky)
- 2–4 more weeks before the new guy is up to speed
- Lower production from the rest of the crew during the transition
- Mistakes and rework from the new guy learning your system
- The risk that other guys follow him out the door

Conservative estimate: **losing one experienced piece rate worker costs you $10,000–$25,000** in lost production, recruiting, training, and rework.

If paying $5 more per unit keeps your best guys loyal for another year, that's the best money you ever spent.

> **Action step:** Look at your crew turnover from last year. Count the guys who left. Multiply by $15,000 (a conservative middle estimate). That's what turnover cost your business. Now ask yourself: would a raise have been cheaper?

## Part 3: Bidding With Your Eyes Open

Here's where we put it all together.

Most contractors bid one of two ways:

1. **From the gut.** "That looks like a $12,000 job." Based on experience, which is fine until the job surprises you.
2. **From the base rate.** "That's 60 squares at $45, so $2,700 in labor." Better, but still missing the real number.

The contractors who consistently profit bid a third way:

**3. From the fully burdened cost.** They know exactly what every unit of production costs them — including the invisible costs — and they price the job with that real number as the floor.

### The Profitable Bid Formula

**Minimum Bid = (Units × Burdened Labor Cost per Unit) + Materials + Equipment + Overhead Allocation + Desired Profit**

Let's run it on that same 60-square roof:

| Line Item | Calculation | Amount |
|---|---|---|
| Labor (burdened) | 60 sq × $60.75 | $3,645 |
| Materials | 60 sq × $125 | $7,500 |
| Dump/delivery | flat | $600 |
| Equipment rental | flat | $200 |
| Overhead allocation | 8% of job | $955 |
| **Total cost** | | **$12,900** |
| Desired profit (20%) | | $2,580 |
| **Bid price** | | **$15,480** |

Now you know your **floor.** Anything below $12,900 and you're losing money. At $15,480 you're making a real 20% margin — not a 20% markup on a number that was already wrong.

### Margin vs. Markup: The Mistake That Kills Contractors

These two words sound the same. They're not, and confusing them costs contractors thousands.

- **Markup:** What you add on top of cost. A 20% markup on $12,900 = **$15,480.**
- **Margin:** What percentage of the final price is profit. 20% margin on the same job = $12,900 ÷ 0.80 = **$16,125.**

That's a **$645 difference** on a single job. Multiply that across 100+ jobs a year and you're leaving $50,000–$60,000 on the table by using the wrong word.

> **Action step:** Check your estimating process. Are you using markup or margin? If you want 20% profit, are you dividing your cost by 0.80 (margin) or multiplying by 1.20 (markup)? Know which one you're doing — and make sure it's the right one.

## Part 4: The Compliance Trap That Can Wipe Out Everything

You've done the hard work. You know your burdened rate, you're paying your guys well, you're bidding with real numbers, and the profit is there.

Now let's make sure nobody takes it away from you.

The federal government and most states have very specific rules about how you pay piece rate workers. And most contractors — even the ones who've been doing this for 20 years — are getting at least one of these wrong.

### Trap #1: Overtime on Piece Rate

If your crew works more than 40 hours in a week, you owe overtime. But here's the part that trips everyone up: **you can't just multiply the piece rate by 1.5.**

The Fair Labor Standards Act requires you to:

1. Add up total piece rate earnings for the week
2. Divide by total hours worked to get the "regular rate"
3. Pay an additional 0.5× that regular rate for every hour over 40

**Example:**

A worker earns $1,800 in piece rate pay and works 50 hours.

- Regular rate: $1,800 ÷ 50 = $36/hr
- Overtime premium: $36 × 0.5 = $18/hr
- Overtime pay: $18 × 10 hours = $180
- **Total owed: $1,800 + $180 = $1,980**

Most contractors either skip this entirely or calculate it wrong. Both are violations.

### Trap #2: Minimum Wage Makeup Pay

If a worker's piece rate earnings divided by hours worked falls below the minimum wage, **you owe the difference.** This happens more often than you'd think — a slow week, bad weather, a job that takes longer than expected.

**Example:**

A worker earns $400 in piece rate pay and works 45 hours in a state with a $15 minimum wage.

- Effective hourly rate: $400 ÷ 45 = $8.89/hr
- Minimum wage: $15/hr
- Shortfall per hour: $6.11
- **Makeup pay owed: $6.11 × 45 = $274.95**

You can't just shrug and say "that's what he earned." You owe the makeup pay. Every single week.

### Trap #3: State-Specific Landmines

Some states have rules that go far beyond federal requirements:

- **California (AB 1513):** You must pay piece rate workers separately for rest periods and recovery periods — you can't bundle it into the piece rate. Violations come with penalties per worker, per pay period.
- **Washington:** Piece rate workers must be paid at least minimum wage for all hours, including non-productive time. Overtime applies to all hours over 40.
- **New York:** Specific pay stub requirements for piece rate workers showing units completed, rate per unit, and hours worked.

The penalties for getting this wrong aren't small. We're talking:

- **Back pay** for every affected worker, going back 2–3 years
- **Liquidated damages** — often double the back pay owed
- **Penalties** — per worker, per pay period, per violation
- **Attorney fees** — theirs and yours
- **DOL investigation** — which often uncovers other issues

One wage claim from one unhappy worker can trigger an audit that covers your entire crew. A $2,000 mistake can become a $200,000 problem.

### Trap #4: Recordkeeping

The FLSA requires you to keep detailed records for every piece rate worker:

- Hours worked each day and each week
- Total piece rate earnings
- Regular rate calculation
- Overtime hours and pay
- Total compensation paid

If you're tracking this on paper or spreadsheets, ask yourself: could you produce these records for every worker for the last three years if the Department of Labor showed up tomorrow?

> **Action step:** Pick one crew member at random. Can you pull up their hours worked, pieces completed, regular rate, and overtime calculation for any week in the last year? If the answer is anything other than an immediate yes, you have a recordkeeping gap that puts your business at risk.
